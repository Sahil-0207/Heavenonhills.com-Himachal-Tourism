Kullu

Intro

Kullu, known as the 'Land of Gods' (Devtas) for its many village deities, is a quiet town that attracts travellers, connoisseurs, photographers, filmmakers, and honeymooners all year. It is a refreshing escapade for tired souls as a gateway to the popular destination of Manali.
Founded as a capital of Kullu in the year 1660, today the sprawling township serves as the administrative headquarter of the valley district.
The intricate web of valleys, thick forests, gushing streams and rivers, rich orchards, and the forbidden heights of the Greater Himalayas for a gorgeous backdrop is truly breathtaking. Myths and stories enrich the intensely devout beliefs of a simple people, which may be witnessed throughout the valley's many festivals.
In October, when the Kullu Dussehra festival takes place for a week, the peaceful town comes alive. An assemblage of Devtas - village deities carried on palanquins - graces the event, celebrating the triumph of good over evil.

Language Spoken: Hindi, English, Punjabi are spoken and understood by those engaged in tourism. Locals speak Pahari.

Clothing essentials: Tourists are advised to carry cotton clothing in summer, light woollen in spring and autumn and heavy woollen in winter.

Destinations

Himalayan National Park


This national park, which is surrounded on three sides by the Greater Himalaya mountains, maintains a rich range of species and is one of the greatest places for western Himalayan flora and fauna enthusiasts. The park is also a haven for Western Tragopan, Monal, and other uncommon birds, as well as the rare and endangered Himalayan Thar, Himalayan Brown Bear, Snow Leopard, and Blue Sheep. This national park, which is flanked on three sides by the Greater Himalaya mountains, is one of the best destinations for western Himalayan flora and wildlife aficionados. The park is also home to the rare and endangered Himalayan Thar, Himalayan Brown Bear, Snow Leopard, and Blue Sheep, as well as the Western Tragopan, Monal, and other unique birds.

Manikaran


A Gurdwara was established in tribute to Guru Nanak Dev, the founder of the faith, who is reported to have visited the region with five disciples. It is located gently on the bank of the river Parvati, with lofty mountains rising over the valley. The compound's hot sulphur spring is medicinal and has been proven to heal a variety of disorders, including skin problems. Every year, a great number of pilgrims visit the site. Manikaran, located 45 kilometres from Kullu, is easily accessible by private or public transportation.

Kasol


Kasol, which stretches along the banks of the Parvati River, is a popular tourist destination for both local and international visitors. To fulfil the growing demand of vacationers visiting the area, a picturesque village that is now split by a bridge between old and modern townships has just lately developed to include a growing number of guesthouses, campers, and hotels.

Jalori Pass


Jalori, a wind-swept dominating pass, separates the Satluj and Beas catchments. The panoramic picture includes distant snow-capped Himalayan mountains, the Great Himalayan National Park's thick woods, and river basins on both sides of the ridgeline. The coolness in the air makes for excellent walking territory in Jalori. A day trek to Serolsar Lake is an unforgettable experience.

Sahoja


Shoja's scattered nature, contemplative solitude, and sheer raw beauty satisfy the craving for a peaceful mountain getaway. Travelers return year after year because of the attractively designed cottages nestled among apple orchards with a panoramic view of the valley. Shoja also serves as a starting point for hikes to Jalori Pass, Serolsar Lake, Takrasi, and Khanag. This charming village is located off the major Kullu-Ani route, which passes via Jalori Pass. Regular buses are provided on the route by both public and private carriers. There are also taxis available.

Angling in Banjar


Angling is a fun activity that involves matching your fishing abilities to a fish's agility by catching one and then releasing it back into the water. The clean waters of the snow-fed River Tirthan are home to a large number of trout. Angling along the river sections near Banjar is a fun filled activity to partake in if you want to put your fishing skills to the test. Buses run frequently to Banjar. Taxis are readily available for hiring as well.

Malana


Malana, an ancient Himalayan community nestled deep in the Parvati valley, had long been a self-contained democratic society. Jari is now connected to the settlement by a road that branches off the Bhuntar-Manikaran route. Trekkers can access the settlement by passing across the Chanderkhani Pass. The residents, who are deeply attached to their religious beliefs and holy groves, worship Jamlu Devta, the local deity, whose divine laws are still enforced by oracles and which regulate the village's institutions. Many trekkers flock to Malana because of its ancient traditions, remoteness, high altitude (2650 m), and beautiful splendour.
